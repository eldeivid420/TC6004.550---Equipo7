## Abstract 
Flood prediction in Mexico City has emerged as a critical challenge due to the interaction of climatic, hydrological, urban, and infrastructural factors that increase territorial exposure and vulnerability. Current emergency management practices remain largely reactive, relying on citizen reports to guide response and maintenance actions, which often results in delayed intervention, economic losses, traffic disruption, and inefficient allocation of resources. Prior research has addressed this problem through multiple perspectives, including land-use and runoff-based hydrological vulnerability assessments, spatial flood susceptibility models, hydrological-hydraulic simulations, institutional risk atlases, and nature-based solutions. Collectively, these studies demonstrate that flood risk in the Valley of Mexico depends not only on precipitation intensity, but also on infiltration loss, urban expansion, topography, drainage performance, land-use change, subsidence, and basin degradation.

Despite these contributions, important limitations remain. Much of the existing literature emphasizes risk assessment and scenario simulation rather than operational prediction, while several approaches rely on return periods or synthetic rainfall, limiting their applicability to real-time forecasting and early warning. In this context, this work proposes the development of a predictive flood model for the Valley of Mexico that integrates meteorological, hydrological, territorial, and urban information within a unified framework. The proposed dataset combines temporal indicators, meteorological and soil-moisture variables, spatial and administrative descriptors, and lag-based aggregated features, enabling the representation of both current environmental conditions and antecedent hydrological dynamics. By shifting from descriptive and simulation-oriented approaches toward predictive modeling, this study seeks to support more effective urban risk management, territorial planning, and early warning systems in Mexico City.
